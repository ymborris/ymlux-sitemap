## I. Cultural Context: From Ancient Pun to Regal Authority

The Gallic rooster's journey begins in antiquity. In Latin, *gallus* signified both the inhabitants of Gaul (ancient France) and the rooster itself—a linguistic coincidence Roman conquerors exploited as mockery. Yet the Gauls, fierce and vigilant, reclaimed the symbol. Over centuries, it evolved into an emblem of courage, alertness, and resilience.

Christian symbolism deepened its resonance: the rooster's dawn crow recalled Peter's repentance and the triumph of light over darkness, gracing church steeples as weather vanes of faith and watchfulness. During the French Revolution, republicans elevated it as a populist icon of the people—proud, opinionated, ready to defend liberty. Napoleon favored the eagle, but the rooster endured, adorning coins, memorials, and war emblems after World War I as a mark of unbreakable spirit.

In modern France and its global diaspora—in Toronto, Montreal, Chicago, Los Angeles, Miami, Houston—the rooster embodies quiet national strength. It appears on the French national football team's kit, a badge of heritage worn with understated pride rather than ostentation.
